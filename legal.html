International Driving Permit Disclaimers

Nature of International Driving Permits

An International Driving Permit (IDP) is a translation of your domestic driver's license and is not a standalone driving license. The IDP must always be carried together with your valid domestic driver's license when driving abroad. The IDP does not grant you the right to drive; it merely translates the information on your existing license.

Acceptance and Recognition

While our IDPs are designed to comply with international conventions and are generally accepted in over 150 countries, we cannot guarantee acceptance by every authority, car rental agency, police officer, or institution. Acceptance may vary based on:

  • • Local laws and regulations that may change without notice
  • • Individual policies of car rental companies
  • • Discretion of local authorities and law enforcement
  • • Changes in international agreements or bilateral treaties
  • • Political or diplomatic relations between countries
  • • Emergency situations or temporary restrictions

Legal Compliance Responsibility

It is your responsibility to:

  • • Research and comply with the driving laws of your destination country
  • • Verify current IDP requirements before traveling
  • • Ensure your domestic driver's license remains valid
  • • Carry both your IDP and original license while driving
  • • Obtain appropriate insurance coverage for international driving
  • • Follow all local traffic laws and regulations

Validity Limitations

Your IDP is only valid while your domestic driver's license remains valid. If your domestic license expires, is suspended, or is revoked, your IDP becomes invalid immediately. We are not responsible for monitoring the status of your domestic license or notifying you of its expiration.

Jurisdictional Variations

International driving requirements vary significantly by country and jurisdiction. What is acceptable in one country may not be acceptable in another. Factors that may affect IDP acceptance include:

  • • Whether the country is a signatory to the 1949 Geneva Convention or 1968 Vienna Convention
  • • Bilateral agreements between your home country and destination country
  • • Local interpretations of international agreements
  • • Recent changes in local laws or regulations
  • • Political or diplomatic relations
  • • Emergency situations or temporary restrictions

We strongly recommend verifying current requirements with the embassy, consulate, or relevant authorities of your destination country before traveling.
